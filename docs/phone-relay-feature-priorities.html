Product Memo

Build Phone Relay around Android control, capture, and reliable local workflows.

Phone Relay should not become a general phone companion app. Its strongest lane is narrower and more defensible: mirror an Android device on Mac, control it cleanly, install builds, capture proof, and keep the connection trustworthy.

Local-first No account No cloud relay Mac-native Android workflow tool

Recommended positioning

Mirror, control, install, screenshot, and record your Android phone from your Mac.

Support it with the trust line: No account. No cloud relay. Your Android screen stays between your phone and Mac.

The product focus

The most important decision is what Phone Relay refuses to be. Do not compete as a messages, calls, contacts, and full companion suite. Compete as the most practical Mac tool for controlling and capturing Android locally.

Primary user

People who need to operate an Android phone from a Mac

Developers, QA testers, support teams, product managers, demo creators, and power users who need the phone visible, controllable, and recordable.

Primary job

Turn the phone into a reliable Mac workflow surface

The product should make it easy to connect, mirror, control, install, capture, and troubleshoot without making users think about adb.

Main promise

Fast local control without a cloud account

The trust story is not secondary. It is the reason Phone Relay can feel cleaner than broad device-management products.

How to decide what ships

Use these principles when a feature is tempting but could pull Phone Relay away from its focused direction.

✓
Does it help users operate Android from Mac?

If the feature does not improve mirroring, control, install, capture, recording, or troubleshooting, it is probably out of scope.

✓
Does it reduce setup uncertainty?

Reliability and diagnosability are product features. A clear fix beats a hidden technical capability.

✓
Does it turn an existing capability into a workflow?

Profiles, demo mode, bug capture, install status, and capture history all make current capabilities easier to use.

✓
Does it preserve the local-first trust story?

A feature that requires accounts, cloud relay, or broad personal data access should clear a very high bar.

Features to avoid for now

These ideas may sound useful, but they move Phone Relay toward a companion app. That makes the product harder to explain, harder to build, and harder to trust.

Do not prioritize

SMS, calls, and contacts

These features imply a personal communication hub. They bring permission complexity, edge cases, and support expectations that do not match the focused screen-control product.

Do not prioritize

Virtual webcam and microphone

These are interesting, but they introduce separate OS-level complexity and privacy expectations. They are not needed to win the mirroring and capture lane.

Do not prioritize

Full Android file manager

Focused folder import is enough at first. A full file manager creates a new product surface and competes with tools that specialize in transfer.

Do not prioritize

Broad device companion dashboard

A phone status dashboard pushes the product toward general companion behavior. Keep the main UI centered on connection, mirror, control, install, and capture.

Recommended next actions

The next step should create visible product clarity without needing a large technical rewrite.

Product copy

Update website and onboarding language

Use “Mirror, control, install, screenshot, and record your Android phone from your Mac” as the plain-language promise. Use the local-first trust line near setup and privacy sections.

Implementation

Start with Connection Health and Mirror Profiles

These two features strengthen the core product immediately. They also create a better foundation for Demo Mode and Bug Capture Mode.

Bottom line

Phone Relay should win by being the cleanest local Android control and capture tool for Mac. Build reliability, workflow packaging, and capture depth before adding any broad phone-companion features.
